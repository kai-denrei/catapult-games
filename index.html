CATAPULT

1972 — 2009

This is a lineage, not a list. Each entry preserves a single input gesture — the act, not the art, of throwing a rock at a wall. It is built in vanilla because the originals were too.

1976 — Artillery

Mike Forman · BASIC, Creative Computing · direct ancestor: War 3 / Artillery 3 (FOCAL, c. 1972).

The first published catapult duel printed in a magazine. The interaction is the act of typing two numbers and pressing return. The arc is a result, not an animation.

Gesture preserved: numeric input of angle and power as the entire interaction.

1982 — Smithereens!

Ed Averett · Magnavox Odyssey² · the only real-time entry; the front-view castle framing that Defender of the Crown would later inherit.

Both catapult arms swing on their own clock. The player does not aim — the player chooses a moment. The opponent does the same. There is no turn to wait for.

Gesture preserved: real-time symmetric dueling — hold to lock the arm at the current angle, release to fire.

1986 — Defender of the Crown

Kellyn Beeck · Cinemaware, Amiga 1986 (DOS / ST / Mac / C64, 1987) · the “interactive movie” frame.

Cinemaware turned the catapult sideways and put the player behind it. Aim is removed; only the duration of the pull-back survives. The manual says to aim high first, then lower — the body of the shot lives in the length of the press.

Gesture preserved: analog pull-back tension as power — hold to wind, release to fire, angle fixed by geometry.

1991 — Gorillas / Scorched Earth

Gorillas (QBasic, IBM) and Scorched Earth (Wendell Hicken) · the modern archetype of the side-view duel and the basis for Worms and its descendants.

The side view returned; the turn returned; the terrain became destructible. Wind, last shown as decoration in Artillery, now matters: it accelerates the projectile horizontally for the whole flight.

Gesture preserved: side-view turn-based duel with destructible terrain and wind compensation.

2009 — Crush the Castle / Angry Birds

Crush the Castle — Joey Betz / Armor Games, Flash, April 2009 · Angry Birds — Rovio, December 2009 (Rovio cited Crush the Castle directly).

Defender’s pull-back was rotated ninety degrees onto a side-view stage, then bound to a slingshot. The aim vector is dragged out; the projectile launches along its inverse. The blocks fall, plausibly.

Gesture preserved: drag-back slingshot release — the aim vector is dragged out, the projectile launches along the inverse.

Sources: Creative Computing Sep–Oct 1976; Odyssey² cartridge documentation; Cinemaware’s Defender of the Crown manual; QBasic Gorillas.bas source; Crush the Castle, Armor Games.

Acknowledged dead ends: no level editor, no cross-iteration scoring, no auto-play on scroll, no pastiche of original art. Each widget stands alone.
